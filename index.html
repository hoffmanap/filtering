01 — Overall verdict

Not "no filtering" — weak, uneven filtering

The claim that filtering "isn't happening at all" overstates it. Cheaper, older tracts show mild relative price convergence in repeat sales. But every operational signal — vacancy, tenure churn, new-vs-resale spread, permit volume — points the same direction: the mechanism is weak and getting weaker, concentrated in a shrinking part of the market.

02 — Geography

The discount is real, and it's concentrated in the historic core

Median sold price per square foot ranges from $115 in the central/south-central core to $188 in far-east and west-side new-construction zips — roughly a 60% spread that has held steady quarter over quarter.

MLS closed sales, Apr 2024–Aug 2025, by zip code. Bars sorted low to high; color intensity = tract minority population share (HMDA, 2024).
03 — Demographics

Price tracks vintage more than it tracks race — but they're entangled

County-wide HMDA purchase loans (2024): median property value falls from $285K in the least-minority tracts to $215K in the most-minority tracts. But 79911 — 90% minority, new construction — sells at $188/sqft, higher than several majority-white legacy zips. Age of housing stock, not demographic composition per se, is the sharper predictor; they correlate because the historic core is both the oldest stock and the highest-minority area.

Hispanic/Latino applicants were denied credit at 29.3% in 2024 vs. 20.5% for non-Hispanic applicants — a real access barrier on top of the price gap, which matters because filtering only works if buyers can actually move into the newly-affordable stock.
04 — The mechanism

Vacancy is rising exactly where filtering should be tightening it

Splitting tracts into value tiers and tracking ACS 5-year vacancy estimates since 2010: if filtering worked, the cheapest tier's vacancy should be low and stable — a sign that older stock keeps getting absorbed by new buyers. Instead it climbed from 8.4% to a peak of 11.7% (2019) and has only partly recovered to 10.3% (2024), while mid- and high-value tiers returned to or below their 2010 levels.

Lowest value tier Mid value tier Highest value tier
ACS DP04 5-year estimates, tracts matched to Housing Characteristics value tiers (156 of 158 tracts). Vacancy gap between lowest and highest tier: +0.8pp in 2010 → +3.1pp in 2024.

Rental conversion tells a similar story: renter-occupied share rose least in the cheapest tier (+2.7pp, 2010–2024) versus the mid (+4.5pp) and highest tiers (+4.8pp). The classic filtering channel — aging owner-occupied homes converting to rentals as they move down-market — is running slower, not faster, in the tracts that most need it.

05 — Supply pipeline

The city is building roughly half as much as it used to

Filtering needs a steady flow of new construction pulling higher-income buyers out of existing homes. That flow has been cut nearly in half, and what's left is increasingly single-family only.

City of El Paso annual building permits, 1988–2025. Single-family share of permits rose from ~65–70% (2010s) to 85–89% (2020s) — multifamily production, the more direct channel to renters, shrank even faster than the overall total.

Why this targeting: rehab & anti-vacancy tracts vs. city average

Rehab & anti-vacancyHigh priority

Vacant/blighted property registration fees + targeted rehab loans

Rising, sticky vacancy in the lowest-value tier (10.3% in 2024, vs. 7.2% in the highest tier) looks like disinvestment, not efficient turnover. A registration fee on long-vacant residential parcels raises the cost of land-banking or neglect; paired with low-interest rehab loans or a tax abatement for occupancy, it pushes stock back into active use rather than sitting empty.

TargetCentral/south-central core: zips 79905, 79915, 79907, 79903, 79904; tracts flagged "Lowest Demand" / "Lowest value tier" in the Housing Characteristics file.
Zoning reformHigh priority

Legalize duplexes/triplexes and ADUs in the historic core

Renter-share growth is weakest exactly where filtering should convert aging owner-occupied homes to rentals fastest. Gentle upzoning (missing-middle infill, ADUs) gives owners of aging homes a path to add units and capture rental income, instead of the home simply sitting owner-occupied, deteriorating, or vacant.

TargetSame core tracts as above, plus any adjacent "Average Demand" tracts with older (pre-1970) vintage per Housing Characteristics.
Permitting & supplyHigh priority

Streamline approvals and cut minimum lot/parking requirements for infill multifamily

Total permits fell ~45% from the 2010s pace, and multifamily's share of that shrank even faster. Rebuilding the multifamily pipeline — specifically infill 2–20 unit product inside already-serviced areas — is the most direct way to add supply at the price points that could actually filter, rather than adding only greenfield single-family at the edge.

TargetCity-wide policy change, but prioritize approval-timeline relief for parcels inside 79901–79907, 79924/79925 corridors with existing infrastructure.
Fair lending accessMedium priority

Down-payment assistance + credit-building programs tied to the affordable core

A 29.3% denial rate for Hispanic applicants vs. 20.5% for non-Hispanic applicants means even where cheap stock exists, buyers who'd benefit most are disproportionately blocked from moving into it. Targeted down-payment assistance or CDFI-partnered underwriting closes the access gap that price alone doesn't fix.

TargetBuyers purchasing in the identified low-value/high-minority tracts (79905, 79915, 79907, 79903) specifically, not city-wide.
Growth managementMedium priority

Impact-fee or inclusionary mix requirement on new far-east/west subdivisions

New construction (79938, 79928, 79934, 79911, 79932) is where nearly all permit activity already lands, and its price premium over resale keeps widening. Requiring a modest share of smaller/attached units in new subdivisions there won't fix the core, but it slows the divergence at the top and adds starter-home supply the resale market currently lacks.

TargetFast-growth peripheral zips only — not a city-wide inclusionary mandate, which would just suppress the one part of the pipeline that's still building.
Net read: the highest-leverage moves are demand-side interventions in the core (rehab incentives, missing-middle zoning) paired with rebuilding the multifamily supply pipeline city-wide. Anything aimed only at the periphery, where the market is already active, will miss the tracts where the data show filtering is actually stalled.
