A data investigation · El Paso, Texas

Is El Paso's housing actually getting cheaper as it gets older?

Economists call this "filtering" — the idea that as new homes get built for buyers with money, the older homes they leave behind become affordable for everyone else. We pulled apart a decade of sales records, mortgage data, census surveys, and permits to find out if it's actually happening here.

First, the basics

What does "filtering" even mean?

It's the theory that housing markets sort themselves out over time, like a hand-me-down chain for an entire city.

1. A new home gets built. A higher-income family buys it.
→
2. Their old home is now empty. It's older, so it's cheaper — a middle-income family moves in.
→
3. That family's old home is even older and cheaper — a lower-income family gets it.

Repeat that chain for 40 years across a whole city, and in theory, the oldest housing stock should be the most affordable — not falling apart, just handed down and still in use. That's the promise. Here's what we actually found in El Paso.

The short answer

Barely — and where it's closest to working, it isn't helping much.

El Paso's cheapest, oldest neighborhoods aren't rotting. But they're not being smoothly absorbed the way the theory predicts either. Vacancy is stuck high. Rental turnover is sluggish. New construction is pulling further away in price instead of feeding the chain underneath it. The clearest evidence points to a weak, stalled version of filtering — not a healthy one, and not a total absence either. Here's the data, piece by piece.

Where — the geography

A tale of two El Pasos

Home prices aren't random across the city — they line up almost perfectly with how old the neighborhood is. The historic core costs about 60% less per square foot than the newest suburbs, and that gap hasn't budged in two years of sales data.

Median sold price per square foot by zip code, MLS closed sales, April 2024–August 2025. If filtering were pulling these numbers together over time, we'd expect this gap to be narrowing — instead it's been flat.

This is exactly the geography that matters for filtering: the historic core — roughly the neighborhoods in and around downtown and South-Central El Paso — is where the oldest housing stock lives, and where the theory says affordability should be flowing to. The fast-growing suburbs to the far east and west are where nearly all of the new construction actually gets built. Filtering requires those two things to be connected — new supply up top loosening up the older stock underneath. Scroll down to see whether that's actually happening.

The case FOR filtering

The one piece of good news

We tracked 1,609 homes that sold twice — once years ago, once again in 2022 — so we could measure real price growth on the exact same houses, not just averages.

Average annual price growth on the same homes, sold twice between 1996–2020 and again in 2022, grouped by neighborhood value. Source: Repeat Sales file, single-family homes only.

On a percentage basis, homes in El Paso's cheapest neighborhoods actually gained value a little faster than homes in the priciest ones — 7.2% a year versus 6.6%. That's a real, measurable sign that the affordability gap isn't accelerating in the wrong direction. If this were the whole story, we'd call it a point in filtering's favor.

But percentages can be misleading when the starting prices are so different. Keep scrolling.

The case AGAINST filtering

Same percentage, very different dollars

A 7.2% gain sounds better than 6.6%. But 7.2% of a cheap home is a small number, and 6.6% of an expensive home is a big one.

Homes in El Paso's priciest neighborhoods build wealth about three times faster in real dollars than homes in the cheapest ones — even though the percentage growth rates are almost the same. The gap isn't closing. It's just moving in percent instead of dollars.
The case AGAINST filtering, continued

The cheap neighborhoods have a vacancy problem, not an absorption success

If filtering were working, the oldest, cheapest homes should be in high demand — snapped up as soon as they become available. Instead, vacancy there has stayed stubbornly high for 15 years.

Cheapest third of tracts Middle third Priciest third
Census vacancy rate estimates by neighborhood value tier, 2010–2024. The priciest and middle tiers are back near their 2010 levels. The cheapest tier never fully recovered from its 2019 peak.

This is the opposite of what a healthy filtering market looks like. Empty homes sitting in the cheapest neighborhoods for years at a time is a sign of neighborhoods being avoided, not neighborhoods absorbing move-down demand.

The case AGAINST filtering, continued

New construction is pulling away from everything else

If filtering were working, new and older homes should trade at prices that move together over time. Instead, new construction has become a different, faster-moving market of its own.

The case AGAINST filtering, continued

And there's simply less new housing to fuel the chain

Filtering needs a steady supply of new homes at the top to push the whole chain downward. El Paso is building roughly half as much housing as it used to.

Single-family permits Multifamily & other permits
City of El Paso annual residential building permits, 1988–2025.

Multifamily housing — the type most directly connected to renters, who are usually the ones filtering is supposed to help — has shrunk even faster than the overall total. Single-family homes now make up 85–89% of permits, up from about two-thirds a decade ago.

The case AGAINST filtering, one more piece

We traced actual moves — using real sale prices this time

Filtering theory makes a specific prediction: when someone moves out of a home, whoever moves in next should be paying less. We tested this using mover records that show exactly where 9,463 households moved from and to, matched against real MLS sold prices instead of modeled estimates — a meaningfully stronger version of this test than our first pass.

This version matches each mover's own former and current address directly against real MLS sales — blocked on an exact house number match, then fuzzy-matched on street name to allow for typos and abbreviations (e.g. "DR" vs. "Drive"). It's a stricter, more precise method than our first pass, and it lands on essentially the same conclusion with more data behind it: people are moving up in El Paso — just not obviously by freeing up a bargain for whoever comes next.

A plausible reason why

The discount on old homes may just be too small to bother fixing them up

Here's one explanation for why the historic core stays vacant and undervalued instead of getting renovated: buying old and fixing it up may not actually save enough money to be worth the effort, once you account for what renovation costs.

The math: a typical old-core home sells for about $33/sqft less than a comparable new-construction home — roughly a $45,000 discount on a typical 1,360 sqft house. Even a light cosmetic refresh runs $20,000–$82,000 nationally, and a genuine mid-range remodel runs $54,000–$204,000. So the entire savings from buying old barely covers the lightest possible touch-up, and doesn't come close to funding a renovation that would make the home comparable to new construction. If the gap were wider — say, if new construction commanded a much bigger premium — buying and fixing up an old home would make a lot more financial sense, and we'd expect to see more of it happening. That's a plausible, if indirect, explanation for the persistent vacancy and disinvestment in El Paso's oldest neighborhoods: not that no one wants to live there, but that the numbers don't clearly reward fixing up what's already there.

Adding it up

The evidence, side by side

One real signal points toward filtering working. Five separate, independent signals point toward it stalling. That's why the honest answer is "barely" rather than a clean yes or no.

✓ Points toward filtering

  • Percentage price growth in the cheapest neighborhoods (7.2%/year) has kept pace with — and even slightly beaten — the priciest ones (6.6%/year) over the long run.
  • Movers overall are trading up: 71% moved to a more expensive home ($250k → $310k median, real MLS prices), confirmed on a house-number-verified match. Mobility itself isn't broken.

✗ Points against filtering

  • Dollar-value growth in the priciest neighborhoods is roughly 3× faster than in the cheapest ones — the real wealth gap is widening even as percentages look similar.
  • Vacancy in the cheapest third of tracts is still above its 2010 level, while other tiers have recovered.
  • The new-vs-resale price gap grew from 11% to 17% in three years — new construction is a separate, faster-moving market, not feeding the chain below it.
  • New housing permits have fallen roughly 45% since the 2010s, cutting off the supply that filtering depends on.
  • The handful of traceable moves where the same home changes hands twice show no clear discount passed to the next occupant.
A plausible reason why: the discount for buying an old home instead of new (about $45,000 on a typical historic-core house) barely covers even a light cosmetic refresh, let alone a real renovation. If new construction cost a lot more relative to resale, fixing up the old stock would pencil out financially — right now, for most buyers, it doesn't.

Put simply: El Paso's oldest, cheapest neighborhoods aren't collapsing, and the theory isn't completely dead here. But nothing in this data shows filtering actively working as an engine of affordability right now — it looks more like a slow, stalled version of the process, propped up mostly by low starting prices rather than healthy neighborhood turnover.

Where each policy is aimed, in plain terms

  • Vacant property & rehab — the historic core, where vacancy is highest and values lowest. Registration fees on empty homes plus rehab loans to get them occupied again.
  • Missing-middle zoning — similar core tracts, already more renter-occupied than average. Legalize duplexes/triplexes/ADUs so aging homes can add units instead of sitting still.
  • Multifamily infill permitting — the oldest-vintage third of tracts citywide. Streamline approvals for small multifamily buildings on already-serviced land.
  • Fair lending access — same core tracts. Hispanic/Latino applicants were denied credit at nearly 1.5× the rate of non-Hispanic applicants citywide in 2024; down-payment assistance targets that gap directly.
  • Inclusionary mix — the fast-growing eastern and western suburbs, where nearly all new supply already lands. A modest share of smaller/attached units there slows the growing new-vs-resale price gap.
